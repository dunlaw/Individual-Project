Notes for guidance on final year projects 
Final year projects provided by the Department of Informatics can vary dramatically in 
their subject matter from software-based projects to media creation projects. Thus, 
guidance from one project to the next will necessarily be different. However, there are 
many aspects that all projects must share to achieve a good mark. 
MANAGEMENT AND PLANNING - When involved with any project it is important to 
manage your time and resources efficiently. One of the tasks in the first weeks of your 
project should be to produce a timetable for your project. This will usually be in the 
form of a “Pert” or “Gantt” chart and will show the amount of time spent on each activity. 
It should also include dates when important milestones should take place e.g. 
completion of background research stage, start of prototyping, completion of interim 
report (week 7) etc. 
I would recommend putting a calendar event in your diary so that you weekly know 
to work on your project.  
Note: the interim report contains the  
 Introduction 
 Professional Issues 
 Background Research 
 Requirements Specification 
 Planning chapter 
Although it is hard at the start of the project to estimate how long particular stages of 
the project will take it is important that you do produce a timetable to guide you. 
PHYSICAL RESOURCES - Early on in your project you will be making a decision on 
the physical resources that you need for your project e.g. which computer. Whether 
you choose to use the Department’s machines or your own PC you must take the 
utmost care to make frequent backups (I suggest using OneDrive or GitHub) of your 
application and dissertation. 
I would advise you to work on university computers because this gives you access 
to some technical support that might be useful. 
It would help if you got into the habit of saving work every 5 minutes while working 
on your projects.  
It would help if you gave files appropriate names – this does make it easier 
Loss of work due to computer problems and your failure to make proper back-ups is 
not eligible as exceptional circumstances. 
PROJECT STRUCTURE - your project should contain the following activities. 
Background literature review - this is a review of a reasonable portion of the available 
literature that is relevant to your chosen project. This literature might be in the form of 
books, conference and journal papers, magazine and newspaper articles, web pages 
etc. This literature review will enable you to create the academic justification for your 
project and project choices. 
Your literature review may cover many areas, but should ideally include background 
for your project idea and a justification of the software and process you plan to use.  
Below are some examples of the areas you would need to review for particular projects: 
If your goal is to develop an application over the web you would need to have a 
review of the following:  
• Similar web-based applications – pick two or three to review and discuss their 
functionality, advantages and disadvantages. 
• A review of some appropriate methods for implementing the application on the web 
e.g. languages, libraries, development tools etc. This should be accompanied by a 
justification of the specific development stack that you have chosen for your project. 
• A review of relevant methodologies that are applicable to the specification, design, 
implementation etc. of your web-based application along with a justification for your 
choice of methodology. 
If your goal is to develop a 3D game, you would need to have a review of the 
following:  
• A critique of a few similar games that will help you decide what 
functionality/gameplay to put into your game. Pick two or three to review and discuss 
their functionality, advantages and any limitations – you can include game review 
information in this critique. 
• How games of the type/genre you intend to implement are produced at the 
moment.  e.g. a review of development platforms/languages (i.e. Unity, UnReal, 
Java, C++, etc.). This should be accompanied by a justification of the specific 
development stack that you have chosen for your project. 
• A review of relevant methodologies that are applicable to the specification, design, 
implementation etc. of your game along with a justification for your choice of 
methodology. 
If your project is a video using complex visual effects techniques, you would need 
to have a review of the following: 
• A review of similar effects shots. 
• Different methods of implementing visual effects e.g. methods of combining 3D 
animation with live action along with justification of your software choice. 
• The possible methodologies used to design visual effects and videos containing 
effects along with a justification for your choice of methodology. 
Production - once you have chosen the most appropriate methodology, you can then 
use it to produce your project. This involves the whole production cycle from feasibility 
and specification to design, implementation, testing and evaluation. 
Testing - this should follow a thorough plan to make sure that your application contains 
no programming or conceptual errors and that it is functionally correct. i.e. parts of the 
application perform as expected, all errors and invalid inputs are caught. The strategy 
for your tests should be linked to your requirements. 
Ethical review: 
You will need to evidence that you are working in an ethical way and reflect on this 
in your report. You must make sure all ethical clearance is secured before doing any 
user testing.  
Evaluation - Evaluation of the project normally requires showing it to the prospective 
end users/viewers and gaining some feedback on the project’s suitability, ease of use, 
functionality etc. Coming at the end of testing (when time is often short) it is easy to 
miss this component, but it is extremely important for real products and for obtaining 
top marks. If you plan to use a user evaluation then you will need to get ethical 
approval for your evaluation – see information here. This needs to go in the appendix 
of your interim report. 
You should also produce an evaluation of the project as a whole for your dissertation 
(this would normally go in your conclusion). This will include a review in retrospect of 
how appropriate your actual system and methodology choices were. 
DISSERTATION WRITING - it is suggested that this activity takes part to some extent 
throughout the project. e.g. you can write up the results (possibly just in draft) of your 
literature survey once it is completed and whilst you are designing the project. It is 
always a good idea (but hard in practise) to keep detailed notes on what you have 
done, this will help tremendously when you start to document your project in the 
dissertation.  
Last year, some students lost marks for relying too heavily on tools like ChatGPT when 
writing their reports. It's important that your work reflects your own understanding so 
remember that we want to hear about your project from your own words. This is not 
something that generative AI models do very well. 
If you're unsure or would like support, please don’t hesitate to reach out. You're 
always welcome to submit drafts and discuss your report with me at any stage of the 
process.

Your dissertation should generally have the following chapters: 
 Abstract – a few paragraphs concisely describing your project, i.e. what you did 
and what the results were. 
 Introduction – general introduction to the area of your project 
 Background Research/Literature Review/Requirements Analysis – depending 
on the project this is likely to be either a review of existing systems/web
sites/video effects or coverage of the research literature that is the basis of your 
work (or possibly a combination of both). Along with a justification of the 
software you have chosen. 
 Professional Considerations – A short review of how the relevant BCS criteria 
apply to your project and a discussion of the ethics surrounding your usability 
evaluation. 
 Requirements – a list of functional and non-functional requirements for your 
project 
 Design – an overview of the code structure and interface design for your project 
or a storyboard or similar for an effects project. 
 Implementation – this is the core chapter and should describe the project 
components that you have produced. You can refer to code in this chapter (if 
you have produced code for your project), but it should not be a line-by-line 
description of your code and should be a more high-level description of the 
functionality which gets across the complexity of what you have produced. 
 Testing – description of how you have made sure that your work is as bug free 
as possible. 
 Evaluation – preferably a user evaluation of the “usability” of your system. 
 Conclusion – should cover 3 components: 
1. How successful your project was, i.e. did it fulfil your requirements and meet the 
project goals 
2. Were there any key issues i.e. how might you do things differently if you could 
complete the project from scratch again 
3. Future work – what would you do if you had more time? 
References – these should use a recognised style and be cited from the appropriate 
place in the text - http://www.sussex.ac.uk/skillshub/?id=251 
Appendices – any supporting material which does not fit in the main text, e.g. additional 
design documents, user evaluation questionnaires etc.  
Remember: Your project is a substantial part of your Degree, manage it well and you 
are likely to get a good mark, but above all this is your chance to work in an area in 
which you are interested and enjoy, make the most of it. 
We will schedule regular meetings to support your project but you can email me if you 
have questions.  
Good Luck.
